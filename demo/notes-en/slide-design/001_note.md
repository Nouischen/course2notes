# The three fundamentals of layout

> [!NOTE] Slide Design, Illustrated (sample) · Unit 1

> [!KEY] In one sentence
> Great-looking slides rest on three basics anyone can master: **contrast to set hierarchy, alignment to create order, and white space to give the message room to breathe**.

> [!KEY] You'll learn
> - How contrast tells the eye what matters most
> - How alignment makes a page feel instantly clean
> - Why white space is a tool, not wasted space
> - The common beginner mistake for each of the three

## Notes

### The three basics at a glance
You don't need to be a trained designer to make slides that look professional. Getting three fundamentals right does most of the work. The illustration below shows how they combine: contrast pulls the eye to the one thing that matters, alignment settles everything onto shared edges, and white space keeps it all from feeling crowded.

![The three fundamentals of layout: contrast sets hierarchy, alignment brings order, white space lets the focus breathe](slides/principles.jpg)

| Principle | What it does | A quick win |
|---|---|---|
| Contrast | Ranks what matters — the eye goes to the boldest thing | Make the key line big and colored; shrink the rest to gray |
| Alignment | Creates order and a sense of intention | Line every element up to a shared invisible edge |
| White space | Lets the focus stand out | Widen the margins; resist filling every corner |

### Using each principle
- **Contrast** — the key point should be big, bold, and colored; secondary information small, thin, and gray. Widen the gap between them rather than making everything the same weight.
- **Alignment** — line elements up along the same invisible edge: left to left, center to center. Instantly the page reads as deliberate rather than accidental.
- **White space** — keep generous margins and space between elements. A crammed layout looks cheap and tires the audience before they've read a word.

> [!TIP] Change one thing first
> If a slide feels off but you can't say why, start with contrast: push the key line up two sizes and mute everything else to gray. That single move fixes more slides than any other.

### Common mistakes
> [!WARN] The three ways slides go flat
> - Everything the same size — the audience doesn't know where to look.
> - Elements centered but at different widths — the page looks crooked even though it's "centered."
> - Content shoved to the edges — nothing has room to breathe.
