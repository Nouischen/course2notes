# Data visualization: pick the right chart

> [!NOTE] Slide Design, Illustrated (sample) · Unit 3
> [!KEY] In one sentence
> A chart exists to **make one conclusion obvious in a second**, so you choose the chart type from the point you're making, not from the data you happen to have.
> [!KEY] You'll learn
> - How to match a chart type to your goal
> - Three moves that make any chart hit harder
> - Why the title should state the conclusion
> - The key vocabulary of clear data visuals

## Notes

### Choose the chart from the goal
Before picking a chart, decide what the audience should see: are you comparing sizes, showing a trend over time, showing a share of a whole, or showing a relationship between two variables? The goal points to the chart, not the other way around.

| You want to show | Use | Watch out for |
|---|---|---|
| Compare sizes | Bar chart | Sort high-to-low for easy reading |
| Change over time | Line chart | Keep it to three or four lines |
| Share of a whole | Pie or stacked bar | Avoid pie beyond ~5 slices |
| Relationship between two variables | Scatter plot | Highlight the group you care about |

### Make the chart hit harder
- **Label the conclusion** — don't title a chart "Revenue"; title it "Q3 revenue up 28%." The title should carry the point.
- **Mute the rest, highlight the point** — color the one bar you mean and gray out the others, so the eye lands where you want.
- **Delete the clutter** — gridlines, heavy borders, and extra tick marks usually add noise, not meaning.

> [!TIP] Title-first test
> Write the chart's title as a full-sentence conclusion first. If you can't, you don't yet know what the chart is for — and the audience won't either.

> [!QUOTE] The test of a good chart
> A good chart needs no explanation — the audience reaches the conclusion on their own.
