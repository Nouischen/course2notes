Time Management in Practice

Sample course · 3 units · 3 notes

1.1 Why you always feel short on time

Course: Time Management in Practice (sample)

Key points

  • Running out of time usually isn't about hours — it's about scattered attention and no priorities.
  • Most people overestimate what they can do in a day and underestimate what they can do in a week — so they're always behind.
  • The first move isn't doing more; it's deciding what NOT to do.

Notes

Three real time thieves

  • Switching cost: every interruption costs ~10+ minutes to get back into flow. It looks like "just a second," but the loss is huge.
  • Fake productivity: busy all day, replying to lots of messages — while moving nothing that actually matters.
  • No boundaries: if anyone can reach you anytime, your day fills up with other people's urgencies.

One mindset shift

You don't "have no time." You just haven't decided whether this thing is important enough.

Takeaways

  • Make a not-to-do list today: three things you'll stop or hand off.
  • Turn off notifications; keep only the truly urgent sources.

1.2 The Eisenhower Matrix: separate "urgent" from "important"

Course: Time Management in Practice (sample)

Key points

  • Urgent ≠ important. Most people spend the day on "urgent but not important," and keep pushing "important but not urgent" away.
  • What actually shapes your life is Quadrant 2 (important, not urgent): health, relationships, learning, long-term bets.
  • The goal: grow Quadrant 2, shrink Quadrant 1, delete 3 and 4.

Notes

The four quadrants

QuadrantTypeWhat to do
1Urgent & important (crises, deadlines)Do it now — but ask why it became urgent
2Important, not urgent (planning, learning, exercise)Schedule it and protect it
3Urgent, not important (most meetings, noise)Delegate, decline, or batch
4Neither (doomscrolling, busywork)Cut it

Key insight

  • A lot of Quadrant 1 fires are just Quadrant 2 work you skipped. Invest in Quadrant 2 early and you fight fewer fires later.

Takeaways

  • Put three "important, not urgent" tasks straight onto your calendar this week.
  • Next time someone hands you a task, ask: which quadrant is this?

1.3 The Pomodoro technique & deep work

Course: Time Management in Practice (sample)

Key points

  • Deep work — long, uninterrupted, focused stretches — is what produces high-value output, and it needs to be protected on purpose.
  • The Pomodoro is the easiest way in: 25 minutes focused, 5 minutes rest. Use rhythm to beat distraction.
  • Two to four real deep-work blocks a day already beats most people.

Notes

How to run a Pomodoro

  1. Pick one task, set 25 minutes, and do only that.
  2. If another thought pops up, write it on paper and come back later — don't jump out now.
  3. When the timer ends, take 5 minutes and leave your seat.
  4. After 4 Pomodoros, take a longer break (15–30 minutes).

Three conditions that make deep work happen

  • Fixed time slot: same time each day so your brain forms the habit.
  • Remove temptation: phone away, notifications off, only the tabs you need.
  • A clear output: before you start, write down what "done" looks like for this block.
Focus is a muscle: at first you can only hold 25 minutes; with practice you can dive for 90.

Takeaways

  • Schedule one 25-minute Pomodoro tomorrow morning for your most important task.
  • Keep a "distraction parking" sheet for thoughts that show up mid-session.

Slide Design, Illustrated

Sample course · 3 units · 3 notes

2.1 One idea per slide

Course: Slide Design, Illustrated (sample)

Key points

  • One slide should carry one message. Cram in more and you've said nothing.
  • The audience can only "read" or "listen" at once — the slide supports your talk, it isn't the script.
  • Write the slide's one-sentence conclusion first, then decide what goes on it.

Notes

Why one idea per slide

  • Short-term memory holds only 3–4 chunks at a time. Five bullets and they remember none.
  • Dense ≠ professional. It makes people anxious and glaze over.

How to do it

  1. For each slide, write "what should the audience remember" in one line.
  2. Delete everything unrelated to that line.
  3. Make that line big and put it where the eye lands first.
If a slide needs to say three things, split it into three slides. Slides are cheap; attention isn't.

Takeaways

  • Open your last deck and find slides with 3+ points — split them.

2.2 Contrast, alignment, white space

Course: Slide Design, Illustrated (sample)

Key points

  • Good-looking slides rest on three basics: contrast (rank what matters), alignment (create order), white space (let it breathe).
  • You don't need to be a designer — getting these three right already lifts the quality a lot.
  • White space isn't wasted space; it's what makes the point stand out.

Notes

How to use the three basics

  • Contrast: the key point should be "big, bold, colored"; secondary info "small, thin, gray." Widen the gap — don't make everything the same.
  • Alignment: line elements up along the same invisible line. Left to left, center to center, and the page instantly looks clean.
  • White space: keep margins generous and space between elements. A crammed layout looks cheap and tiring.

Common mistakes

  • All text the same size → the audience doesn't know where to look.
  • Everything centered but different widths → it looks crooked.
  • Content pushed to the edges → no room to breathe.

Takeaways

  • Take one slide and do just one thing: bump the key line up two sizes and shrink the rest to gray. See the difference.

2.3 Data visualization: pick the right chart

Course: Slide Design, Illustrated (sample)

Key points

  • A chart exists to make the conclusion obvious in a second, not to dump numbers.
  • Different goals need different charts: compare sizes, show a trend, show a share, show a relationship.
  • Decide "what should the audience see" first, then choose the chart.

Notes

Choosing a common chart

You want to showUseWatch out
Compare sizesBar chartSort high-to-low for easier reading
Change over timeLine chartKeep it to 3–4 lines
Share of a wholePie / stacked barAvoid pie beyond ~5 categories
Relationship between two variablesScatter plotHighlight the group you care about

Three ways to make a chart hit harder

  • Label the conclusion: don't title it "Revenue"; title it "Q3 revenue up 28%."
  • Mute the rest, highlight the point: color the one bar you mean; gray out the others.
  • Delete clutter: gridlines, borders, extra ticks can all go.
A good chart needs no explanation — the audience gets it on their own.

Takeaways

  • Take one chart in your deck and rename its title to the conclusion it's making.

Personal Branding 101

Sample talk · the first step to being seen for your expertise

In one sentence

A personal brand isn't "packaging yourself" — it's making the right people, at the right moment, think of you for the right reason — built on consistent, genuine, compounding expression of your expertise.

TL;DR

  • Personal brand = your expertise × your recognizability × other people's trust — all three matter.
  • You don't need to be an influencer. The goal isn't "lots of people know you," it's "the right people trust you."
  • Content should compound: every post and share raises the credibility of the next one.
  • Don't chase hype or force a persona. Lasting beats loud.

Notes

1. First, decide: for whom, solving what

  • The starting point isn't "I want to be famous" but "who do I want thinking of me, and for what?"
  • Narrow the audience and topic: rather than "the person who knows everything," be "the first person people think of in one area."

2. Recognizability: be memorable

  • A consistent topic, tone, and look — even a signature phrase — build memory hooks.
  • Consistency beats flashiness: show up the same every time, and trust accumulates.

3. Trust: show, don't claim

How others see you is more convincing than how you describe yourself.
  • Real cases, results, and testimonials beat any self-description.
  • Collect them as you go: what you did, who you helped, and what they said.

4. A compounding content rhythm

  • Rather than one big post now and then, publish small ones steadily.
  • Start with the questions people ask you most — each question is one piece of content.

Takeaways

  • [ ] In one line, write: who do you want thinking of you, and for what.
  • [ ] List the 5 questions people ask you most — those are your first 5 topics.
  • [ ] Start a running list of your results and testimonials you can cite anytime.

Worth remembering

A personal brand isn't one viral moment — it's long-term, genuine, compounding trust.
